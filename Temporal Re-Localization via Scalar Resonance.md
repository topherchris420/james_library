## Abstract—Temporal re-localization is often dismissed as a
violation of causality; however, in the context of Dynamic Location
Theory, it emerges as a specific, constrained case of spatial re-
localization. This paper integrates phenomenological descriptions
of temporal displacement with a rigorous mathematical frame-
work of scalar field resonance. We demonstrate that temporal re-
localization is not the transport of matter along a closed timelike
curve, but rather the stabilization of a system’s localization
eigenstate at a temporal coordinate t′̸ = t. By extending the
frequency-dependent localization operator L to the temporal
domain, we derive a quadratic energy scaling ansatz E ∝ (∆t)2
that places macroscopic temporal displacement beyond current
energy scales (> 1045 J). This framework resolves classical
paradoxes by treating temporal position as a resonant state of
coupled matter-scalar fields, distinguishing between physically
prohibitive matter transport and feasible information retrieval.

## I. INTRODUCTION
In conventional physics, objects evolve along worldlines
governed by the geodesic equation. Temporal re-localization—
deviation from this monotonic progression—is typically
treated within General Relativity as requiring Closed Timelike
Curves (CTCs) generated by exotic matter [2]. While mathe-
matically valid, such solutions often lead to causal paradoxes.
This paper proposes an alternative mechanism rooted in
Dynamic Location Theory [1]. Rather than bending spacetime
geometry, we posit that an object’s location in time is an
emergent property of resonance with a background scalar
field (e.g., vacuum expectation values). If temporal position
is spectral rather than purely geometric, then “movement”
in time becomes a problem of frequency tuning rather than
superluminal transport.
Understanding the physical limits of temporal displacement
has implications for quantum computing architectures, infor-
mation theory, and the foundations of thermodynamics. This
approach offers a novel pathway to unify the phenomenology
of temporal engagement with the rigorous dynamics of quan-
tum information theory.
